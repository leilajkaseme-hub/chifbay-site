Monte Festival & Columbus Festival 2026: Madeira's Late-Summer Celebrations

11 July 20266 min readFunchal · Madeira

Once the Wine Festival winds down, Madeira isn't finished celebrating. Two very different events carry the island through late summer and into autumn: a centuries-old religious pilgrimage above Funchal, and a costumed re-enactment of Columbus's voyages on the neighbouring island of Porto Santo. Here's what each one is, when they happen in 2026, and how to fit them into a trip.

What is the Monte Festival in Madeira?

The Monte Festival — Festa de Nossa Senhora do Monte — is Madeira's most important religious festivity, honouring the island's patron saint in the hillside parish of Monte, 5km above Funchal. It centres on the Church of Nossa Senhora do Monte, whose twin white towers overlook the capital, and combines nine days of novenas with a full weekend of processions, fireworks and street celebration. Locals travel from across the island, and many walk up the steep steps to the church barefoot as an act of devotion.

When is the Nossa Senhora do Monte Festival in 2026?

Novenas — evening prayers held in the run-up to the main event — take place from 5 to 13 August 2026. The festival itself falls on 14–15 August, with the image of the Virgin carried through streets decorated in flowers on the evening of the 14th, followed by an all-night arraial (street party) with food stalls, music and fireworks into the early hours of the 15th, which is also Portugal's Feast of the Assumption public holiday.

What happens during the festival?

The streets around the church are laid with tapetes — carpets of flowers and coloured salt — and stalls sell grilled meat, corn and local wine long into the night. The religious procession is solemn, but it gives way to a genuinely festive atmosphere once the image returns to the church. Monte is also home to Madeira's famous wicker toboggans, the carros de cesto, which run year-round from the church square down to Funchal — worth doing before or after the festival crowds arrive, since the queue moves faster on an ordinary day.

What is the Columbus Festival in Porto Santo?

The Columbus Festival is an annual historical re-enactment held in Vila Baleira, the capital of Porto Santo, Madeira's smaller neighbouring island. For three days the town's streets fill with residents in 15th-century dress, staging markets, street theatre, music and mock battles that recreate the era when Christopher Columbus lived on the island. A full-scale replica of his ship, the Santa Maria, anchors offshore as the festival's centrepiece.

When is the Columbus Festival in 2026?

The festival runs for three days in mid-September, though the exact dates shift slightly from year to year — recent editions have landed anywhere between the 17th and 21st of the month. If the festival is the reason for your trip, check the current dates on visitmadeira.com a few weeks ahead of travel rather than relying on last year's calendar.

How do you get from Madeira to Porto Santo?

Two options run daily: the passenger ferry from Funchal, which takes around two hours fifteen minutes and carries vehicles as well as foot passengers, or a short flight from Madeira Airport, which takes roughly 15–20 minutes. Both increase in frequency over summer, and it's worth booking the ferry ahead during festival week since capacity is limited and locals travel over for it too.

Why did Columbus end up on Porto Santo?

Columbus lived on Porto Santo in the 1470s after marrying Filipa Moniz Perestrelo, daughter of the island's first governor, Bartolomeu Perestrelo. Historians credit that period — surrounded by Atlantic sailors, charts and stories of currents and driftwood arriving from the west — with shaping the ideas that eventually led to his 1492 voyage. The festival isn't a stretch of local pride; the connection is genuinely documented.

Can you combine both festivals in one trip?

Not in the same week — Monte falls in mid-August and Columbus in mid-September — but both sit inside Madeira's warm, low-crowd stretch between the peak summer months and the wetter winter season, so either works well as the anchor for a longer autumn trip. Many visitors base themselves in Funchal for the Monte Festival, then add a two-day side trip to Porto Santo a few weeks later for the Columbus celebrations and the island's long sandy beach — the only one of its kind in the archipelago.

What's the best way to see Funchal's harbour during festival season?

Late August and September give some of the calmest sea conditions and warmest water of the year, which makes it a good stretch for time on the Atlantic between land-based festivities. A private boat trip with Chifbay gets you off Funchal's crowded waterfront during festival week, with the town's lights and fireworks visible from the water on festival nights without needing to fight the crowds on shore.

One festival looks inward, up the hill to a church older than most of Madeira's roads. The other looks out to sea, to the voyage that started three miles from where you're standing.

Whether you catch the flower-strewn streets of Monte in August or the costumed market of Vila Baleira in September, both festivals are a reminder that Madeira's calendar runs on more than beaches and boat trips — though there's always time for those too.
