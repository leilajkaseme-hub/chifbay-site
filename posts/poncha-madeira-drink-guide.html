What Is Poncha? Madeira's Traditional Drink, Explained

2 July 20266 min readFunchal · Madeira

Order a drink anywhere in Madeira and someone will eventually steer you toward poncha. It's the island's most recognisable drink, and also one of the most misunderstood by first-time visitors — stronger than it tastes, and more specific than a generic "rum cocktail." Here's what it actually is.

What is poncha made of?

Poncha is made from three core ingredients: aguardente de cana (a clear spirit distilled from sugarcane, grown mostly on Madeira's south coast), honey, and freshly squeezed citrus. Lemon is the traditional base, though orange and tangerine variations are widely served, especially in winter when tangerines are in season. The mixture is beaten — not shaken or stirred — with a wooden tool called a caralhinho, a stick with a small propeller-like end, until it turns pale, frothy and slightly cloudy. There's no fixed recipe; every bar and every family has its own ratio, but the balance of sharp citrus, sweet honey and raw spirit is what defines it.

Where does poncha come from?

Poncha's roots are in Câmara de Lobos, the fishing village just west of Funchal that also produced the sugarcane spirit itself. Fishermen mixed it before heading out to sea, partly for warmth and partly as a folk remedy against seasickness and colds — sugarcane and citrus were both cheap and plentiful on an island built around cane fields. What started as a working drink for people who spent their lives on the water became, over generations, Madeira's unofficial national drink, and Câmara de Lobos is still considered its spiritual home.

Is poncha strong?

Yes — aguardente de cana usually sits around 40% ABV, on par with standard spirits like rum or whisky. The honey and citrus make it dangerously easy to drink quickly, since the sweetness masks the alcohol, but the strength is real. Locals treat it as a slow, sociable drink rather than a shot — sipped over conversation, not downed. If it's your first poncha, pace yourself and eat something alongside it; espetada and bolo do caco are the classic pairing.

How do you order poncha like a local?

Specify your citrus. The default is lemon (poncha de limão), but asking for orange (de laranja) or, if it's in season, tangerine (de tangerina) marks you as someone who knows the drink rather than a first-timer. In Câmara de Lobos's traditional poncha bars, it's still mixed to order in front of you with the caralhinho — worth seeking out over a pre-mixed version. Poncha is also a fixture of Madeiran hospitality on the water: it's common on private boat trips along the south coast, including as part of the sunset and half-day charters we run at Chifbay, where a chilled glass in hand while Cabo Girão slides past is about as Madeiran as an afternoon gets.

What's the difference between poncha and a caipirinha?

Both drinks are built around sugarcane spirit and citrus, which leads visitors to assume they're interchangeable — they're not. A caipirinha uses Brazilian cachaça, muddled with granulated sugar, lime and ice, served cold and diluted. Poncha uses Madeiran aguardente de cana, sweetened with honey rather than sugar, and beaten rather than muddled, with no ice added. The result is smoother, less diluted, and noticeably stronger glass for glass, with a honeyed warmth that a caipirinha doesn't have.

Can you buy poncha to take home?

Bottled aguardente de cana and pre-mixed poncha kits are sold in supermarkets, the Mercado dos Lavradores in Funchal, and most souvenir shops on the island, making it one of the easiest Madeiran flavours to bring home. It won't taste quite the same as a glass beaten fresh with real honey and citrus at the source, but it travels well and keeps indefinitely unopened — a fair souvenir for anyone who wants a taste of the island back home.

Poncha isn't a cocktail you order to look adventurous. It's what fishermen drank to get through a cold morning at sea — and it still tastes like it.

Like most things in Madeira, poncha makes the most sense in context: on a warm evening, after a day outdoors, ideally with the Atlantic somewhere in view. Try it on land in Câmara de Lobos first, then on the water — it tastes different both ways, and you'll want to know which one you prefer.
