What Is Espetada? Madeira's Traditional Beef Skewers

15 July 20266 min readFunchal · Madeira

Espetada is Madeira's signature dish: large chunks of beef, seasoned with nothing more than coarse salt and garlic, skewered on a fresh bay-laurel branch and grilled over an open fire until charred outside and pink within. Here's what's actually in it, how it's cooked, and where to eat it properly.

What is espetada?

Espetada is a Madeiran dish of skewered, grilled beef — the word simply means "on a skewer" in Portuguese. Unlike a mixed kebab, Madeiran espetada is almost always beef alone, cut into generous chunks so the outside chars while the centre stays tender. It's less a recipe than a technique: good beef, minimal seasoning, real fire, and a stick of bay laurel doing quiet work in the background. It's served across the island in tascas, roadside grills and family kitchens, and it's usually the first dish visitors are told to order.

What meat is used in espetada, and how is it cooked?

Traditional espetada uses a lean, well-marbled cut of beef — often rump or topside — trimmed into cubes roughly 4–5cm across. The only seasoning is coarse sea salt and crushed garlic, sometimes rubbed on hours ahead so it draws out and reabsorbs moisture rather than just sitting on the surface. The meat is threaded onto the skewer and cooked directly over hot wood embers or charcoal, turned by hand so each side gets brief, intense heat. There's no sauce and no marinade beyond salt and garlic — the char and the smoke are the flavour.

Why is espetada served on a bay laurel skewer?

The skewer itself, a fresh branch of louro (bay laurel), is what separates espetada from ordinary grilled beef. As the branch heats over the fire, its sap and oils lightly perfume the meat, adding a faint herbal note you can't get from a metal skewer. In many restaurants the espetada is served still on its branch, hung from a small hook or iron stand directly over your plate so it drips onto bread or potatoes below as you carve pieces off — part meal, part performance.

Where can you eat the best espetada in Madeira?

O Cesteiro and O Lagar, both in Estreito de Câmara de Lobos just above the coast, are the two restaurants most locals point to first — no-frills grill houses built almost entirely around this one dish, with queues most evenings. Closer to the water, several restaurants along the Câmara de Lobos waterfront and in Funchal's Zona Velha old town serve espetada too, often alongside bolo do caco (garlic bread) to mop up the juices. If you're spending an afternoon on the water with us, we sometimes bring grilled skewers and local cheese aboard for exactly this reason — it's the kind of food that works as well on deck as it does at a roadside grill, and it's part of what makes a private boat day with Chifbay feel like a genuine taste of the island, not just a scenic cruise.

What do you eat and drink with espetada?

Espetada is almost always served with bolo do caco — a flat, griddle-baked bread rubbed with garlic butter — and often fried corn (milho frito) or simple boiled potatoes. To drink, poncha is the classic pairing: its sharp citrus and honey cut cleanly through the richness of the grilled beef. A young, fruity Madeira red or a cold Coral beer both work too, and it's common to see all three on the table at once. Portion sizes are generous by design — espetada is meant to be shared, cut straight off the branch onto everyone's plate.

Is espetada available outside Madeira?

Grilled beef skewers appear in mainland Portugal and Brazil too, but the specific combination of salt-and-garlic-only seasoning and a bay-laurel skewer is distinctly Madeiran, and harder to find authentically elsewhere. Some restaurants abroad label any skewered grilled meat "espetada," but without the laurel branch and the open-fire char, it's closer to a generic churrasco than the real thing. If you want the genuine version, Madeira — and specifically the grill houses around Câmara de Lobos — is still the place to have it.

No sauce, no marinade, no shortcuts — just fire, salt and good beef on a branch of bay laurel.

If poncha is Madeira's drink, espetada is its meal — simple enough to make at home, but rarely as good as it is grilled over open coals a few steps from where it was raised. Order it with bread, a cold drink, and no cutlery you don't need.
