What to Pack for Madeira

13 July 20266 min readFunchal · Madeira

Madeira confuses packers more than most islands — it's subtropical at sea level and near-alpine on the peaks, sometimes on the same day. Here's what actually earns a place in your suitcase, and what you can safely leave at home.

What clothes should I pack for Madeira?

Light layers year-round, plus one warm layer regardless of season. Daytime temperatures in Funchal sit between 19-25°C for most of the year, so t-shirts, linen shirts and shorts or light trousers cover the coast. Evenings cool down, especially outside summer, so pack a light jumper or cardigan. Madeira is also more formal than a typical beach destination — smart-casual clothing works better than swimwear-as-outerwear if you're heading into Funchal for dinner.

Do I need to pack for rain in Madeira?

Yes, in every season. Madeira's terrain creates sharp microclimates — the south coast around Funchal is drier and sunnier, while the north coast and mountains catch far more rain and cloud, often on the same afternoon the south stays clear. A packable, breathable rain jacket is more useful than an umbrella, which is unwieldy on windy cliff roads and levada paths. Rain is most frequent from October to March, but a sudden shower at altitude is possible even in July.

What shoes do I need in Madeira?

Bring at least two pairs: comfortable walking shoes and closed-toe hiking shoes or trail runners with real grip. Funchal's pavements are cobbled and can be slippery when wet, and Madeira's beaches and natural pools are volcanic rock and pebble rather than sand, so flat sandals fall short. If you're walking any levada, even an "easy" one, a shoe with ankle support and tread is worth the suitcase space — paths run beside irrigation channels and can be uneven or damp.

Is Madeira warm enough for swimwear year-round?

Yes, though the sea is noticeably warmer from June to October. Sea temperatures range from around 18°C in February-March to 23-24°C in late summer, so a swimsuit belongs in the bag whatever month you visit. Pack a quick-dry rash guard or light wetsuit top if you feel the cold easily or plan to snorkel for long stretches — the Atlantic here is clear but rarely bath-warm.

What should I pack for hiking the levadas and Pico do Arieiro?

Warm layers, a headtorch, and a printed or downloaded SIMplifica booking confirmation, since several of the busiest trails now require a free advance reservation. Pico do Arieiro sits at 1,818 metres and can be 10-15°C colder than the coast, with fog rolling in fast, so a fleece, gloves and a windproof shell matter even in August if you're going for sunrise. For the levadas, bring a small daypack with water, a snack and a torch for the handful of trails that pass through tunnels.

What should I bring for a boat trip in Madeira?

Swimwear, a towel, sunscreen, sunglasses with a strap, and a light windbreaker for the ride out of the marina, where the breeze is stronger than it looks from shore. Reef-safe sunscreen is worth seeking out specifically, since Madeira's marine reserves and coves are part of what make the coastline worth visiting. On a private Chifbay charter, towels, water and refreshments are already on board, so you genuinely only need what you'd wear to the beach — everything else is optional.

What travel documents and practical items do I need?

A valid passport or EU ID card, travel insurance details, and a UK/EU two-pin plug adapter if you're travelling from outside continental Europe. Madeira uses the euro and standard European sockets. A reusable water bottle is genuinely useful — tap water is safe to drink island-wide, and refill points are common in Funchal. Cash is still handy in smaller villages and markets, even though cards are widely accepted in Funchal itself.

Pack for four seasons in one bag, and Madeira will use every item in it — usually in the same afternoon.

If in doubt, pack lighter than you think and layer more than you think. Between the coast, the cliffs and the cloud forest, Madeira rewards travellers who came prepared for all three.
