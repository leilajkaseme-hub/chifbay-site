Top 10 Beaches in Madeira

6 July 20267 min readFunchal · Madeira

Madeira is a volcanic island, not a sand-dune one — most of its coastline is pebble, rock or dark volcanic sand rather than the pale beaches you might expect from a Portuguese island. Here are the 10 beaches and natural pools that actually reward the trip, in the order we'd send a first-time visitor.

Does Madeira have sandy beaches?

Only two beaches on the main island have soft, golden sand, and both are man-made. Praia de Machico and Praia do Calheta had sand imported from the Sahara and shipped in to create the kind of beach the island's basalt coastline doesn't naturally produce. Every other spot on this list is either pebble, dark volcanic sand, or a natural rock pool — which, once you've swum in one, you'll stop minding.

1. Prainha, Caniçal

Prainha is Madeira's only naturally occurring sand beach, tucked into a small cove on the wild east coast near Caniçal. The sand is dark, coarse and volcanic, the cove is sheltered by cliffs on both sides, and the water is calm enough for real swimming rather than just paddling. It gets busy on summer weekends, so aim for a weekday morning.

2. Praia de Machico

Machico's golden, imported sand sits inside a bay closed off by two breakwaters, which keeps the water flat and shallow — the safest swimming beach on the island for families with young children. It's also the most developed, with cafés and sunbeds right along the promenade.

3. Praia do Calheta

On the sunnier, drier west coast, Calheta is Machico's rival for soft-sand family swimming, with two enclosed sandy coves rather than one long bay. It pairs well with a day exploring Madeira's south-west, where the coastline turns dramatically steep just beyond the town.

4. Praia Formosa, Funchal

Formosa is a long pebble beach a short drive west of central Funchal, popular with locals rather than tour groups. It has none of Machico's imported sand, but the trade-off is space, a proper promenade, and sunsets over open Atlantic with no cliffs in the way — one reason a Chifbay sunset cruise often passes just offshore.

5. Ponta do Sol

This small south-coast town has a modest pebble beach and a natural pool at its harbour wall, but the real draw is the setting: a fishing village stacked up a cliff face, with some of the best light on the island in late afternoon.

6. Porto Moniz Natural Pools

At the island's northwestern tip, lava flows created a series of interconnected volcanic pools that the Atlantic fills and refreshes with every set of waves. They're the most famous natural pools in Madeira, with walkways, changing rooms and lifeguards, so expect company in high season.

7. Seixal Natural Pools

Further along the north coast, Seixal's pools get a fraction of Porto Moniz's visitors despite being just as striking, with a natural rock archway framing the entrance to one pool. Combine it with the drive through Seixal's waterfalls-over-the-road stretch, one of the more dramatic pieces of coastal driving anywhere on the island.

8. Doca do Cavacas, Funchal

A natural volcanic pool right at the western edge of Funchal, Doca do Cavacas is the closest thing to a wild swim you'll find within the city limits — a short taxi ride from the marina rather than a road trip across the island.

9. Reis Magos, Câmara de Lobos

A quiet pebble beach just past Câmara de Lobos, largely used by residents rather than visitors. The water is clean and the setting unpretentious, with the colourful fishing boats of Câmara de Lobos harbour visible along the coast.

10. Ribeira Brava

A central south-coast pebble beach with an easy promenade, Ribeira Brava works well as a stop between Funchal and Calheta rather than a destination in itself — pleasant, uncrowded, and a good measure of how much calmer the coast gets once you're outside Funchal.

What about Porto Santo?

If a proper sand beach matters more than anything else, Porto Santo — a 1-hour-20 ferry or 15-minute flight from Madeira — has roughly 9km of continuous golden sand along its entire south coast, the closest thing to a classic beach holiday this archipelago offers.

Madeira doesn't do beach holidays the way Portugal's mainland does — it does coastline, and the coastline is the whole point.

Several of the calmest coves near Funchal — the kind with no car park, no path down the cliff, and nobody else in them — are only really reachable by sea. That's the appeal of seeing this coast from a private boat rather than a rental car: you get to the water Madeira actually looks like in photos, not just the stretch with parking.
