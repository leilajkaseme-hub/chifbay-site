Madeira's Natural Pools: The Complete Guide

27 June 20267 min readFunchal · Madeira

Madeira has no sandy beaches to speak of — but it has something rarer: volcanic lava pools carved by the Atlantic, filled with crystal-clear seawater, and spread across an island that remains one of Europe's best-kept swimming secrets.

What exactly are Madeira's natural pools?

The pools form where ancient lava flows reached the sea and cooled into irregular basalt terraces. Over thousands of years, the Atlantic carved channels and basins into the rock. At high tide, seawater floods in and the walls trap it — naturally filtered, remarkably clear, and sheltered enough to swim in conditions that would be impossible on an open shore. There is no sand underfoot; this is dark volcanic stone, often rough and uneven, which is why water shoes are non-negotiable at every pool on the island.

Where are the best natural pools in Madeira?

Four pools stand out for very different reasons:

Porto Moniz — The island's most famous pools sit at the northwest tip, a two-hour drive from Funchal through mountain tunnels and coastal switchbacks. The main complex is partially developed: basalt walls reinforced for safety, a small café, changing rooms, and a modest entrance fee. The setting is spectacular — the open Atlantic smashes against the outer walls while swimmers float in blue-green calm. For first-time visitors and families with young children, Porto Moniz is the easiest and safest introduction.

Seixal — About 10 km east of Porto Moniz along the dramatic north coast road, Seixal's pools are wilder, free to enter, and arguably more beautiful. A waterfall drops directly into the swimming area from the cliff above. The water is exceptionally clear, cold even in summer, and deep enough to dive — but the rock here is rougher and the swells more unpredictable. Seixal rewards confident swimmers willing to read the sea before getting in.

Doca do Cavacas — The closest quality natural pool to Funchal, about 4 km west of the city centre just past Ponta Gorda. It lacks the drama of the north coast, but it is well maintained with concrete entry ladders, and it is perfectly positioned for an early morning swim before a day in the capital. Local families use it year-round — a reliable sign of quality.

Porto da Cruz — On the northeast coast, Porto da Cruz has a smaller lava pool beside a striking old sugar mill chimney. Quieter and less touristed than Porto Moniz, the village atmosphere and dramatic backdrop make it worth the winding drive for travellers already exploring the east of the island.

Which natural pool is best for families with children?

Porto Moniz is the safest option for young children — the main pools are enclosed, the water stays calmer, and facilities are the most developed on the island. Doca do Cavacas is a solid second choice for families based in Funchal who don't want a full-day drive. Seixal and Porto da Cruz are better suited to older, confident swimmers; the lack of barriers and the unpredictability of north-coast swells make them less appropriate for toddlers or non-swimmers.

Can you swim in Madeira's natural pools year-round?

Yes — and that is one of the pools' key advantages over Madeira's limited beach options. The Atlantic feeds them regardless of season. Water temperature sits around 18–19 °C in winter and 22–24 °C in July and August. Conditions are calmest from June through October, when the sea settles and the north coast is at its most swimmable. In winter, a north-coast swell can make Seixal genuinely dangerous — always check local conditions before entering, and err on the side of caution if the outer rocks are taking big waves.

Are there natural swimming spots only reachable by boat?

Several of Madeira's most secluded lava-rock swimming areas have no road access at all — they sit below cliff faces along the south coast, sheltered from swell, with water clarity that the road-accessible pools rarely match. The only way in is by sea. Chifbay's Hidden Coves Half-Day trip anchors in exactly these kinds of spots: sea-level lava platforms, snorkel-clear water, no other boats, and complete quiet. It is the part of Madeira's coastline that the majority of visitors never see.

What should you bring to Madeira's natural pools?

Pack these before you leave:

  • Water shoes — the single most important item; the lava rock will cut bare feet
  • Mask and snorkel — the basalt underwater at Seixal is worth exploring; visibility regularly exceeds 10 metres
  • Sunscreen and water — shade is scarce on open lava terraces and the Atlantic sun reflects off the rock
  • Cash — Porto Moniz charges a small entrance fee; some car parks on the north coast are pay-and-display
  • A dry bag — spray from the outer walls can drench anything left on the rocks when a wave hits

How to get to Porto Moniz from Funchal

There are two main routes. The faster option takes the Via Expresso (VR1) west from Funchal and then heads north over the mountains via Rabacal or through the Paul da Serra plateau — allow around 1 hour and 15 minutes in light traffic. The scenic option follows the coastal EN101 from Ribeira Brava up the dramatic north coast — allow around 2 hours and be prepared for narrow tunnels and sheer drops. The coastal route is the better drive; the expressway is easier for those with young children or who prefer predictable road conditions. There is no direct public bus from Funchal that covers the full route in a reasonable time; a hire car is the practical choice.

The island's most memorable swims aren't on a beach. They're in a ring of black rock with the Atlantic crashing beyond the wall and cold, clear water below you.

Whatever your swimming ability, Madeira's natural pools offer something the island's famous mountains and levadas cannot: the chance to get in the water. Whether you drive to Porto Moniz for the day or slip into a hidden south-coast cove by private boat, the Atlantic here is worth getting wet for.
