Snorkeling in Madeira: Best Spots & Marine Life

4 July 20267 min readFunchal · Madeira

Madeira isn't a tropical reef destination, but its volcanic coastline hides genuinely good snorkeling: clear Atlantic water, protected marine reserves, and coves you can only reach by boat. Here's where to go, what you'll see, and when.

Is Madeira good for snorkeling?

Yes — with the right expectations. Madeira's underwater landscape is volcanic rather than coral, so the appeal is dramatic rock formations, arches and drop-offs rather than a reef in the tropical sense. Visibility is usually strong, the water is clean, and a handful of protected areas hold a real density of fish, octopus and rays. What makes it work is picking the right spot for the day's sea conditions, which is exactly where a boat has the advantage over a fixed shore entry.

Where is the best snorkeling in Madeira?

Garajau Marine Park, a short distance east of Funchal, is the island's only fully protected marine reserve and the most consistent spot for varied fish life — decades without fishing pressure have let the ecosystem recover. Ponta de São Lourenço, the dramatic rocky peninsula at the island's eastern tip, offers clear water and interesting underwater terrain, though it's more exposed to swell. Closer to Funchal, the small coves around Câmara de Lobos and further west toward Cabo Girão give sheltered, calm entries that are ideal for a relaxed swim with a mask and snorkel. On the north coast, the volcanic pools at Porto Moniz offer a shallower, land-based alternative worth combining with a day of driving.

The advantage of doing this by private boat is that you're not committed to one beach for the whole day. If the swell is up at Ponta de São Lourenço, you simply anchor in a calmer cove on the south coast instead — something no shore-based plan can offer.

What fish and marine life will you actually see?

Around the rocks, expect ornate wrasse, parrotfish, damselfish, bream and salema moving in loose schools, with octopus and moray eels tucked into crevices if you look closely. Rays are a realistic sighting in sandier patches, and barracuda occasionally pass through open water. Further offshore, sightings of manta rays are reported, and the Desertas Islands nearby hold a small, closely protected population of Mediterranean monk seals — among the rarest seals on Earth, though not something you'll typically encounter on a standard snorkeling stop near Funchal.

When is the best time to snorkel in Madeira?

May to October is the clear answer. Sea temperature runs 20–25°C, the Atlantic is at its calmest, and visibility is consistently good. From November to April the water cools to around 17–19°C — still swimmable, and still clear on a calm day, but choppier seas and cooler air make it a less comfortable experience for most people. If you're snorkeling outside summer, a light wetsuit is worth packing.

Is Madeira good for beginner snorkelers?

Yes, if you choose sheltered water. The coves and bays on the south coast, out of direct Atlantic swell, are calm enough for nervous swimmers and children, especially with a life jacket. Ponta de São Lourenço and other exposed north- and east-facing points are better suited to confident swimmers, since currents and swell build quickly there. This is another reason a private boat helps — the crew can read the conditions and choose a beginner-friendly spot rather than gambling on whichever beach you happened to book.

Why is a private boat better than a shore snorkeling spot?

A boat removes the two biggest limitations of shore snorkeling: access and choice. Many of Madeira's clearest coves have no road down to them at all, so a boat is the only way in. And because conditions can vary sharply between the north coast, the south coast and the east tip on any given day, a boat lets you snorkel wherever the water happens to be calmest and clearest that morning, rather than hoping the one spot you drove to is any good. On Chifbay's Hidden Coves Half-Day, this is the whole idea — we anchor in secluded, current-checked water away from other boats, with masks and snorkels on board and no fixed itinerary beyond finding the best conditions of the day.

What should you pack for a snorkeling trip in Madeira?

A well-fitted mask matters more than any other piece of gear — test the seal before you buy or hire one. Reef-safe sunscreen protects both your skin and the water you're swimming in, and a rash vest or light wetsuit adds warmth and cuts glare on the surface. Water shoes are useful if you're entering from rocks rather than a boat ladder, and a dry bag keeps your phone and towel safe on board.

The best snorkeling day in Madeira isn't the one at the most famous spot — it's the one at whichever cove is calm that morning.

Madeira rewards flexibility more than any single "best" location. Whichever spot you choose, check the swell forecast, respect the marine reserves, and — if you can — let a boat do the deciding for you.
