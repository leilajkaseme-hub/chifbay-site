Getting Around Madeira: Buses, Taxis, Car Hire & Cable Cars

7 July 20267 min readFunchal · Madeira

Madeira is a small island with an outsized amount of terrain — steep, folded, and cut by tunnels — so how you get around shapes your whole trip. Here's what actually works in 2026, from the bus you'll take into Funchal to the boat that reaches the parts no road does.

What's the best way to get around Madeira?

There's no single best option — it depends on where you're staying and what you want to see. Funchal itself is compact and walkable; reaching the north coast, the central mountains or the west-coast villages generally means a rental car, a tour, or a taxi for the day. Reaching the coastline itself — the cliffs, caves and coves — means a boat, since most of it has no road at all.

Is it worth renting a car in Madeira?

Yes, if you want to reach places on your own schedule — Pico do Arieiro at sunrise, Porto Moniz's natural pools, or the levada trailheads in the interior. The motorway network connecting Funchal to the airport and the main towns is fast and modern. The trade-off is the older roads: narrow, steep, full of switchbacks and short tunnels, and parking in central Funchal is tight. Book a car in advance in high season, as availability tightens considerably around festivals like the Madeira Wine Festival and New Year.

How does the public bus network work?

Three companies — Horários do Funchal (HF), Rodoeste and SAM — run under a joined-up network sometimes marketed as SIGA, covering the whole island with Funchal as the hub. City routes run frequently on weekdays but drop off sharply on Saturday afternoons and stop almost entirely on Sundays and public holidays, so check the timetable before planning a Sunday excursion. Tickets can be bought straight from the driver in cash, or preloaded onto a rechargeable card if you'll be riding often. Buses reach genuinely useful tourist stops — Monte, the Botanical Garden, Pico dos Barcelos — and some routes even run up to Pico do Areeiro.

Are Uber and Bolt available in Madeira?

Both apps work on the island, but coverage is thinner than you'd expect for a European tourist destination. The regional government has deliberately capped the number of licensed TVDE (ride-hailing) cars, so there are only a few hundred vehicles serving the whole island, concentrated around Funchal, Câmara de Lobos and the airport corridor. That means wait times can stretch, especially at night or outside Funchal — worth knowing before you rely on an app to get you to dinner on time.

What about taxis?

Taxis are metered, widely available around Funchal's centre, the marina and major hotels, and a reliable backup when a ride-hailing app is slow to find a driver. For a full day out — the north coast or a mountain circuit, for example — many taxi drivers will also quote a fixed day rate, which can work out simpler than self-driving on unfamiliar roads.

Are the cable cars useful for getting around?

They're more of an attraction than a transport link, but a genuinely useful one. The Teleférico do Funchal links the seafront to Monte in around 15 minutes, with a round-trip ticket costing roughly €20 for adults (children under 7 ride free, with reduced fares up to 14) — cheaper and faster than driving up and finding parking. Other cable cars connect Fajã dos Padres and the Botanical Garden area; each is a one-off scenic ride rather than part of a day-to-day network.

Is driving in Madeira difficult?

The motorways (the VR and VE roads) are easy — modern, well-signed, and the fastest way between towns. It's the older roads that catch people out: single-lane mountain switchbacks, sheer drops with no barrier, and tunnels that suddenly narrow. It's entirely manageable for a confident, unhurried driver, but it isn't relaxing, which is why many visitors hire a car only for specific day trips rather than for the whole stay.

How do you get from the airport to Funchal?

Cristiano Ronaldo Madeira International Airport sits about 15–20 minutes east of Funchal by road. Taxis and pre-booked private transfers wait outside arrivals; the Aerobus shuttle and regular SAM buses also run into the city centre for a fraction of the taxi fare, with slightly longer journey times.

What's the best way to see Madeira's coastline?

By boat — the coast roads only touch the water at a handful of towns, so the cliffs, sea caves and hidden coves in between are invisible from land. Cabo Girão, one of Europe's highest sea cliffs, only reveals its true scale from below. Get around the island by road. See the island by sea.

Most visitors end up combining two or three of these — a rental car for the mountains, buses or a taxi for Funchal, and a private boat for the coastline. Plan the road part around your schedule, and leave at least one day for the water.
