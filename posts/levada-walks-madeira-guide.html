Levada Walks in Madeira: The Complete 2026 Guide

3 July 20267 min readFunchal · Madeira

Madeira's levada walks changed in a real way this year: the classified trails now cost a small fee and need an online booking before you set off. Here's exactly how the new system works, which walks are worth your time, and what to bring.

What is a levada walk?

A levada walk follows one of Madeira's centuries-old irrigation channels — narrow concrete or stone waterways originally built to carry rain and spring water from the wet north coast to the drier south, where the sugarcane and banana plantations needed it. The paths that run alongside them are flat, shaded, and cut straight through native laurel forest and eucalyptus groves, which makes them some of the gentlest serious hiking in Europe. There are over 200km of levada paths across the island, but only a subset are "classified" PR (percurso recomendado) trails maintained and monitored by the regional forest service, IFCN.

Do you have to pay and book in advance now?

Yes — since 1 January 2026, every classified PR trail requires an online reservation with a fixed 30-minute entry slot, booked through the government's SIMplifica portal. The standard fee is €4.50 per person, dropping to €3 if you're walking with a tour operator that holds a protocol agreement with IFCN. The exception is PR1, the Pico do Arieiro to Pico Ruivo ridge trail, which costs €10.50 from April 2026 given its popularity and the cost of maintaining its tunnels and railings. Children 12 and under don't pay, but still need to be added to the booking, and Madeira residents walk free with a valid resident or citizen card. Unclassified local paths outside the PR network aren't affected and remain free and booking-free.

How do you actually book a slot?

You book directly on SIMplifica (simplifica.madeira.gov.pt), the Madeira regional government's digital services site — search for the PR number of your chosen trail, pick a date and one of the available 30-minute windows, then enter your email and the number of people in your group. You'll get a confirmation to show at the trailhead; screenshot it, since phone signal is patchy or absent in the forest. For the popular routes — PR1, PR6 (25 Fontes) and PR9 (Caldeirão Verde) — slots can sell out three or four days ahead in July and August, so book as soon as your dates are fixed rather than the night before.

Which levada walk should you choose?

It depends on your fitness and how much time you have. Vereda dos Balcões (PR11) is the easiest: a flat, 1.5-hour round trip from Ribeiro Frio through laurel forest to a viewpoint over the central peaks, fine for families and anyone without hiking experience. Levada das 25 Fontes (PR6), near Rabaçal, is the most photographed — a roughly 3-hour round trip ending at a rock amphitheatre with waterfalls dripping into a pool. Levada do Caldeirão Verde (PR9) is longer and more dramatic, threading through unlit rock tunnels (bring a headlamp) to a waterfall pool deep in the Queimadas forest. For anyone properly fit, PR1 between Pico do Arieiro and Pico Ruivo is the big one — a high-altitude ridge trail above the clouds, not a levada in the technical sense, but bookable through the same system and the most physically demanding of the four.

Is the Laurisilva forest worth building a trip around?

Yes. Most levada trails pass through Madeira's Laurisilva — a UNESCO World Heritage-listed laurel forest that once covered much of Southern Europe and North Africa before the last ice age, and now survives almost nowhere else at this scale. It's genuinely primeval: giant tree heather, laurel species found nowhere else on Earth, and a permanent damp, green light that feels closer to a rainforest than a Portuguese island. Even the shorter, easier walks put you inside it within twenty minutes of the trailhead.

What should you pack?

Sturdy closed shoes with real grip — the paths are often narrow, wet underfoot, and run alongside unguarded drops. A headlamp is essential for the Caldeirão Verde tunnels, which are pitch dark for over a hundred metres at a stretch. Pack a light rain layer regardless of season; the north side of the island pulls in far more cloud and drizzle than Funchal ever sees. Bring water, since there's nowhere to refill on most routes, and carry your SIMplifica booking confirmation offline in case signal drops before you reach the trail warden.

When's the best time of year to go?

Levada walking works almost year-round because the forest canopy moderates the temperature, but spring (March–May) brings the most waterfalls and the greenest light, after winter rain has filled the channels. Summer (June–September) is driest and most reliable underfoot, though also the busiest for booking slots. Winter is quieter and often startlingly clear on the south coast, even when the peaks are in cloud — worth checking the IFCN trail status page before you commit, since heavy rain occasionally closes tunnel sections for safety.

The levadas show you Madeira's interior — the wet, green, mountainous half of the island most visitors never expect. The coast is the other half, and it only opens up properly from the water: the cliffs, hidden coves and open Atlantic that no trail reaches.
