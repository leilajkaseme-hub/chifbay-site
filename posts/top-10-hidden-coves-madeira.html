Top 10 Hidden Coves in Madeira

19 July 20267 min readFunchal · Madeira

Madeira's coastline is almost entirely cliff, which is exactly why it hides so many coves — small pockets of clear water backed by volcanic rock, most with no road down to them. Here are the ten worth finding, ranked by how good the swimming is and how hard they are to reach.

1. Fajã dos Padres

A flat green terrace at the base of a 300-metre cliff, planted with vines and banana trees since the 16th century. A cable car connects it to the clifftop road above, but arriving by boat is faster and lets the cove itself — a narrow shingle beach with startlingly clear water — be the whole stop rather than a rushed extra.

2. Calhau da Lapa

A cove beneath Câmara de Lobos known for the shallow troglodyte caves cut into its cliff face by centuries of wave action. There's no path in from land; boats anchor just offshore and it's an easy swim to the cave mouths, which stay cool and shaded even at midday.

3. The coves below Cabo Girão

Europe's tallest sea cliff drops 580 metres straight into a string of small coves at its base, invisible from the clifftop skywalk above. The water here is some of the deepest and clearest on the south coast — this is the stretch most private boat trips build their route around.

4. Praia Formosa's quieter pockets

Praia Formosa is Funchal's main pebble beach and gets busy, but the small rocky inlets at either end of the bay thin out fast once you're a few hundred metres past the main sand. They're an easy add-on for a trip that starts or ends near the marina.

5. Câmara de Lobos' fishing coves

The old fishing village itself sits around a natural harbour, but the smaller coves just west of it — used by local fishermen rather than tourists — are calm, sheltered from swell, and framed by the same black volcanic rock and colourful boats that make the village famous from a distance.

6. Ponta do Sol's hidden bay

Beyond Ponta do Sol's public pier, the coastline folds into a quieter bay backed by terraced hillsides. It's less visited than the village's small beach because it has no direct road access — only a boat or a long scramble gets you there.

7. Reis Magos coves

East of Funchal near Câmara de Lobos, a run of small coves sits below the coastal cliffs, popular with local swimmers but rarely mentioned in guidebooks. The water stays sheltered here even when there's a light swell running further along the coast.

8. Doca do Cavaças

A natural rock pool carved into the base of the cliffs near Funchal, filled and refreshed by the Atlantic at each tide. It's accessible on foot but easy to miss — most visitors pass it entirely on the coastal path above.

9. The coves near Garajau

Garajau's marine reserve protects one of Madeira's richest patches of seabed, and the small coves along this stretch double as some of the island's best snorkeling — schools of parrotfish and the occasional loggerhead turtle are a realistic sighting on a calm day.

10. A cove no one names

The best cove on any given day is often the one a local skipper picks that morning, based on wind direction and swell rather than a fixed list. Madeira's south coast has dozens of small anchorages that never make it into guidebooks precisely because they only work in certain conditions — which is the whole appeal of exploring by private boat rather than a fixed shore excursion.

The best coves in Madeira aren't marked on any map — they're a decision the skipper makes that morning.

Chasing this list by car means long detours, parking that doesn't exist, and cliffs with no way down. By boat, several of these coves fit into a single half-day, with the route adjusted on the water depending on where the sea is calmest.
