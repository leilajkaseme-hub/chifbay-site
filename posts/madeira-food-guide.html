What to Eat and Drink in Madeira

26 June 20267 min readFunchal · Madeira

Madeira has a food culture as distinctive as its landscape — shaped by the Atlantic trade routes, the volcanic soil, and centuries of fishing villages. The island's flavours are unlike anything on the Portuguese mainland. Here's what to seek out.

What is Madeira's most famous dish?

Espetada is the answer: large chunks of beef threaded onto a fresh bay laurel stick and cooked slowly over charcoal. The stick is not decorative — the laurel wood perfumes the meat as the fat renders and drips. Traditionally served hanging from a vertical rack above the table, the juices fall onto bolo do caco bread below. You'll find espetada at practically every celebratory meal on the island, from village festas to Sunday lunches in the hills above Funchal. Order it in the elevated villages of Monte or Camacha for the most atmospheric setting.

What is bolo do caco, and why does every meal come with it?

Bolo do caco is a thick flatbread made from sweet potato and wheat flour, cooked on a flat basalt stone — the caco it's named for. It arrives warm, split in half and spread with garlic herb butter, and it's extremely easy to eat three before your main course arrives. The sweet potato base gives it a slightly golden colour and a pillowy softness no standard bread roll can match. It functions as starter, side dish, street food and sauce-mopper all at once. You'll find it at bakeries across Funchal as a sandwich filled with espetada or cheese. If you only eat one thing in Madeira, make it this.

What should you drink in Madeira?

Poncha comes first. This is the island's genuine local drink — not a tourist confection but something locals actually drink, regularly, in volume. The base is aguardente de cana, a sugarcane spirit distilled on Madeira, mixed with wild honey and fresh lemon juice and beaten with a wooden stirrer until frothy. The original recipe comes from Câmara de Lobos, the fishing village just west of Funchal, where fishermen drank it at sea to stay warm. ABV runs around 40%, but the sweetness and citrus make it deceptively smooth. Drink it at a dedicated poncha bar — not a hotel bar — and start with the classic lemon version before experimenting with passion fruit or tangerine. We keep iced poncha on board for longer boat trips, which is where it tastes best.

What seafood should you try in Madeira?

Three things stand out. Espada (black scabbardfish) is Madeira's signature: a deep-sea creature caught at depths of up to 800 metres in the waters around the island. The fish looks extraordinary when whole — long, jet-black, with a jaw full of needle teeth — but the flesh is white, delicate and sweet. The classic preparation is pan-fried with a crisp skin and served alongside banana fritters, a combination that sounds improbable and tastes excellent. Lapas (limpets) are grilled on the half-shell over charcoal with lemon and butter until just set. They're the Madeiran version of bar snacks and appear on almost every seafood menu along the south coast. Atum (tuna) is the third pillar — landed locally, served grilled or in escabeche (marinated in garlic and vinegar), and a world away from anything tinned.

Is Madeira wine worth seeking out?

Madeira wine is one of the most complex fortified wines in the world, and one of very few wines that genuinely improves after the bottle is opened. It's made in four sweetness levels — Sercial (dry), Verdelho (medium-dry), Bual (medium-sweet) and Malmsey (sweet) — and then aged through a process called estufagem that deliberately exposes the wine to heat and oxygen. The result is virtually indestructible: an opened bottle can sit for months without losing character. Dry Sercial works as an aperitif; Malmsey goes with a slice of local honey cake (bolo de mel). The island's oldest producer, Blandy's, runs cellar tours in central Funchal worth an hour of any afternoon — the oldest barrels date to the 19th century.

Where is the best place to buy local food in Funchal?

The Mercado dos Lavradores (Workers' Market) in the old town is the right answer. Three floors: the ground floor carries spices, spirits and smoked goods; the basement is entirely fish; the upper level is flowers and tropical fruit. Go before 10am for the best selection and the least crowded aisles. The fruit section is worth the visit alone. Madeiran bananas are shorter and sweeter than anything you've tasted — the variety can't travel well, so it never appears in supermarkets abroad. Alongside them: tamarillo, custard apple, pitanga, passion fruit and cherimoya. Buy a bag of bananas. Eat them that day.

Yes — and the setting improves everything. On a private Chifbay trip from Funchal, we bring iced poncha, local cheeses, seasonal fruit and chilled wine as standard. The full-day Coastal Discovery passes close to Câmara de Lobos, the poncha's birthplace and the village Churchill painted from a seafront terrace — you'll see it from the water before any tourist bus finds a parking space. There's nowhere better to understand Madeiran food than with a glass of something cold in hand, looking back at the coast it came from.

The island's flavours make more sense close to where they come from — on the coast, on the water, or above the clouds.

Madeiran food rewards unhurried attention. The same fish on your plate was caught in waters visible from the harbour. The poncha in your glass was distilled a few kilometres away. Eat close to the source, and the island reveals itself one dish at a time.

Frequently asked questions about Madeiran food and drink

What is the national dish of Madeira?

Espetada — beef on a bay laurel stick cooked over charcoal — is the most iconic Madeiran dish, inseparable from island celebrations and Sunday lunches.

What is poncha and where should I try it?

Poncha is a sugarcane spirit mixed with honey and lemon, originally from Câmara de Lobos. Drink it at a local poncha bar, not a hotel. It's stronger than it tastes.

What fish is Madeira known for?

Black scabbardfish (espada), served pan-fried with banana fritters, is the island's most distinctive seafood dish. Grilled limpets (lapas) and fresh tuna (atum) are essential alongside it.

Is bolo do caco gluten-free?

No — bolo do caco contains both wheat flour and sweet potato. It is not gluten-free, though the sweet potato content gives it a distinctly different texture from standard bread.

Where can I taste Madeira wine in Funchal?

Blandy's Wine Lodge in central Funchal offers cellar tours and tastings covering all four styles. Several wine bars in the old town also offer flights by the glass if you want to compare styles without committing to a bottle.
