Cabo Girão From the Sea

21 July 20266 min readFunchal · Madeira

Cabo Girão is a 580-metre wall of volcanic rock that drops straight into the Atlantic, and it only fully makes sense once you're floating at its base looking up. The famous glass skywalk shows you the drop; the water shows you the cliff.

How tall is Cabo Girão, and why does it matter from a boat?

Cabo Girão rises about 580 metres above the sea, making it one of the highest sea cliffs in Europe. From land, that number is abstract — you look down at farmland and water from a platform. From a small boat idling at the base, it stops being a number and becomes a physical presence: a sheer, layered wall of basalt that blocks out a third of the sky. Most visitors underestimate the scale until they're underneath it.

Is the skywalk worth visiting, or should you go straight to the water?

The skywalk is worth it if you want the vertigo of looking straight down — it's a free glass platform in Câmara de Lobos, built out over the edge, and it's genuinely a striking five minutes. But it shows you one thing: the drop. It doesn't show you the cliff itself, its texture, or the way its volcanic strata catch the light. For that, you need to be on the water looking up, not on top looking down. Ideally, do both — the skywalk in the morning, the boat in the afternoon.

What do you see from a boat that you can't see from the top?

From sea level, the layered structure of the cliff becomes readable — bands of darker and lighter volcanic rock stacked over hundreds of thousands of years, with small ledges of green clinging to cracks in the basalt. You also see what's tucked beneath it: Fajã dos Padres, a narrow shelf of banana and vine terraces at the cliff's foot, reachable only by cable car or by sea. Seeing that patch of farmland squeezed between a 580-metre wall and the ocean tells you more about how people have worked this coastline than any viewpoint above ever could.

How do boats reach Cabo Girão from Funchal?

It's a short run along the coast — roughly 20 to 30 minutes west from Funchal Marina, past Câmara de Lobos, before the cliff comes into view. A private boat trip with Chifbay typically combines Cabo Girão with the hidden coves further along the coast, so the cliff becomes one stop on a longer run rather than the whole trip — which suits it, since most people want fifteen or twenty minutes at the base rather than a full afternoon staring at rock.

When is the best time of day to see it from the sea?

Late afternoon is the strongest window. Low, angled sun rakes across the cliff face and picks out its ridges and strata far better than the flat light of midday, when the wall can look like a single dark mass. The sea also tends to be calmer earlier in the afternoon, before any wind that builds over the west coast later in the day — worth factoring in if you're prone to seasickness.

Is it safe to get close to a cliff that size by boat?

Yes, in the conditions boats normally operate in. Experienced skippers read the swell and keep a sensible working distance from the base, close enough for the scale to register without putting the boat near any rockfall zone. Cabo Girão is a stable, long-eroded formation rather than an active hazard — the caution used is standard coastal seamanship, not a special risk unique to this cliff.

You can photograph Cabo Girão from above. You can only understand it from below.

If you only have time to see Cabo Girão once, see it from the water. The skywalk gives you a great photo; the boat gives you the actual size of the thing.
