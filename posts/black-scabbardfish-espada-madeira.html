What Is Espada? Madeira's Black Scabbardfish, Explained

20 July 20266 min readFunchal · Madeira

Order fish anywhere in Madeira and there's a good chance it arrives jet-black, ribbon-thin and fried with a slice of banana on top. That's espada — the island's signature catch, and one most visitors have never heard of until they see it on a menu. Here's what it actually is, why it's almost unique to Madeira, and where to try it.

What is espada?

Espada — peixe-espada preto, or black scabbardfish, in full — is a long, eel-shaped deep-sea fish with jet-black skin, oversized eyes and a mouth full of needle-like teeth. Its scientific name is Aphanopus carbo, and it lives far below the reach of ordinary fishing boats, at depths between 800 and 1,600 metres. On the plate it looks nothing like its raw form: the skin is removed before cooking, leaving a firm white fillet with a mild, slightly sweet flavour and almost no bones to pick around.

Why is black scabbardfish caught almost only in Madeira?

Black scabbardfish is fished commercially in only a handful of places worldwide, and Madeira is by far the best known. The reason is geography: the island sits at the edge of a steep underwater canyon that plunges past 1,000 metres just a few kilometres off the south coast, giving small local boats rare access to deep-water habitat without a multi-day voyage. Most of the world's deep-sea fishing grounds are either far offshore or far colder — Madeira's combination of proximity, depth and mild Atlantic water is unusual, which is part of why the fish has become so tied to the island's identity.

How do Madeiran fishermen actually catch it?

Espada is caught on long-lines — known locally as espinhel — set at night and left to drift with baited hooks spaced along many kilometres of line, then hauled back in by hand or winch the following morning. It's slow, physically demanding work, still done largely by small family-run boats out of Câmara de Lobos, the fishing village just west of Funchal. Because the fish lives under such extreme pressure, its swim bladder and eyes are often visibly affected by the time it reaches the surface — one reason the catch rarely survives being kept alive, and why it's sold and eaten fresh, the same day it's landed.

What does espada taste like, and how is it served?

Espada has a mild, faintly sweet flavour and a soft, flaky texture closer to sole or plaice than to an oily fish like mackerel. The classic Madeiran preparation is filete de peixe-espada com banana: a lightly floured fillet, pan-fried until golden, and topped with a fried banana, sometimes finished with a passion-fruit sauce. It sounds like an odd pairing until you taste it — the banana's sweetness and the fish's delicate flesh work together far better than the description suggests, and it's genuinely one of the island's better-known dishes for good reason.

Where can you eat the best espada in Madeira?

Câmara de Lobos, where most of the fleet lands its catch, has the highest concentration of restaurants serving it hours after it comes off the boat — worth the short drive from Funchal if you want it as fresh as possible. In town, it's a fixture on menus throughout the Zona Velha and at the food stalls inside the Mercado dos Lavradores, Funchal's covered market. Because it's so central to local cooking, you'll rarely need to search hard for it — the harder part is finding a restaurant that doesn't serve it.

Is espada the same fish as the "scabbardfish" found elsewhere?

Not quite. There's a related, silvery species — white scabbardfish (Lepidopus caudatus) — caught in shallower waters around the Mediterranean and mainland Portugal, but it's a different animal from Madeira's black scabbardfish, which lives far deeper and has the distinctive charcoal-black skin. If a menu outside Madeira lists "scabbardfish," it's almost certainly the white variety rather than the island's specific black-skinned catch.

How does espada connect to a day on Madeira's water?

The same Atlantic depths that make espada possible also shape everything else Madeira is known for at sea — the clear water off the south coast, the deep-diving whales, the sheer drop of Cabo Girão straight into the ocean. Seeing that coastline from a private boat trip with Chifbay gives you a sense of just how quickly the seabed falls away here, which is exactly what makes a fish that lives at 1,000 metres reachable from a small boat out of Câmara de Lobos in the first place.

Most deep-sea fish never make it to a plate. Madeira's does — and it's been on the menu for generations.

Espada isn't a novelty dish put on for visitors — it's what Madeirans actually eat, cooked the same way it has been for decades. Order it once with banana, the traditional way, and you'll understand why it's stayed on every menu on the island.
