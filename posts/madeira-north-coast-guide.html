Madeira's North Coast: Porto Moniz, Seixal & São Vicente

17 July 20267 min readFunchal · Madeira

Most visitors see Funchal, the south coast and maybe Cabo Girão, then go home. The north is a different island: wetter, greener, quieter, and shaped by a coastal road that took decades to blast through cliff. Here's what's actually there and how to see it in a day.

What makes the north coast different from the south?

Madeira sits in the path of the north-east trade winds, which push moist Atlantic air straight into the island's northern flank. The result is a coastline that's visibly wilder than Funchal's — steeper cliffs, more waterfalls, denser Laurisilva forest clinging to the slopes, and noticeably more cloud cover and rain, even on days when the south is dry and sunny. Fewer hotels and resorts have been built here, so villages like Porto Moniz, Seixal and São Vicente still function as working fishing and farming communities first, tourist stops second.

How do you get to Porto Moniz?

The fast way is the VE1/VE3 motorway, which tunnels straight through the central mountains from Funchal in around 50 minutes to an hour. The slow way — and the one most people actually choose — is the old coastal road, the ER101, which hugs the cliffs through Ribeira Brava, Ponta do Sol and São Vicente. It's narrower, has short tunnels cut through the rock face in the 1940s and '50s, and stops at viewpoints every few kilometres. Budget close to two hours if you're taking it properly, with time to pull over.

What are the Porto Moniz pools actually like?

At the island's western tip, seawater floods a set of volcanic rock pools that have been partly shaped by lava flows and partly by concrete walkways added for safety. Waves break over the outer rocks and refill the pools, so the water stays cold, clear and constantly moving — closer to swimming in the open Atlantic than a resort pool. There's a small entrance fee, changing rooms and a café overlooking the pools, making it one of the few places on the island where you can comfortably swim in the sea for an hour.

Is Seixal worth the detour?

Seixal is smaller and less visited than Porto Moniz, and that's the appeal. It has one of Madeira's rare stretches of sand — black, volcanic, and framed by green terraced hillsides — plus its own set of natural pools a short walk from the village centre. Just outside Seixal, the Véu da Noiva ("Bridal Veil") waterfall drops straight down a cliff face toward the road; after heavy rain it sprays directly across the tarmac, which is exactly as dramatic to drive through as it sounds.

What's inside the São Vicente caves?

The Grutas de São Vicente e Centro do Vulcanismo are lava tubes formed by volcanic eruptions roughly 890,000 years ago, when molten rock cooled on the outside while still flowing inside, leaving hollow tunnels behind. A guided walk takes you about 1,000 metres through the tubes at a constant cool temperature, and the attached Centro do Vulcanismo explains, with models and displays, how the entire island was built by eruptions like this one over millions of years. It's one of the few places in Madeira where the island's volcanic origin stops being an abstract fact and becomes something you can walk through.

When is the best time to visit the north coast?

Late spring through early autumn — roughly May to September — gives you the best odds of dry roads and calm enough water to swim at Porto Moniz or Seixal. The north catches weather the south coast never sees: it's entirely normal to leave Funchal in sunshine and arrive in Porto Moniz under cloud. Check the forecast for the north specifically before you commit to the drive, and go earlier in the day — cloud tends to build over the mountains by early afternoon.

Can the north coast be combined with a day on the water?

Not on the same trip — the north coast is a road journey, and Madeira's boat charters, including Chifbay's, operate from Marina do Funchal on the south coast, where the water is calmer and the crossing to Cabo Girão, the hidden coves and the dolphin grounds is short. A good rhythm for a longer stay is to give one day to the north by car and a separate morning or afternoon to the private boat trips Chifbay runs from Funchal — two very different sides of the same island, best not rushed into one day.

The north won't show up on most people's three-day itinerary, and that's precisely why it's worth the detour. Pack a jumper even in summer, fill the tank in Funchal, and give yourself the whole day — the road alone is half the reason to go.
