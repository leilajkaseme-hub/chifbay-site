Madeira's Sea Caves: Where to Find Them and How to Explore Them by Boat

10 July 20267 min readFunchal · Madeira

Madeira's coastline looks like a solid green wall from a distance — but get close by sea and it opens up into coves, grottoes and hand-carved caves that no road ever reaches. Here's where to find them and the honest answer on how to actually see them.

Does Madeira have sea caves?

Yes. The island's south coast, from Câmara de Lobos past Cabo Girão toward Ribeira Brava, is volcanic basalt shaped by centuries of Atlantic swell, and it's riddled with caves, overhangs and grottoes at sea level. Some are entirely natural. Others were carved or enlarged by hand long ago — the clearest example being the cluster at Calhau da Lapa, below the cliffs near Fajã dos Padres.

Where are Madeira's best sea caves and hidden coves?

The most concentrated stretch runs between Câmara de Lobos and Ribeira Brava, taking in Cabo Girão — one of the highest sea cliffs in Europe at 580 metres — and the small coastal plains, or fajãs, that sit beneath it. Fajã dos Padres and Calhau da Lapa are the two names worth knowing: both are pockets of flat land squeezed between cliff and sea, both are famous for exceptionally clear water, and both have no direct road access.

What is Calhau da Lapa, and why is it full of caves?

Calhau da Lapa is a remote pebble cove where the cliff face is dotted with roughly 30 troglodyte caves, hollowed out by sea traders and fishermen centuries ago as shelters and stores when this coast was a working stretch of Madeira's maritime trade. A handful have since been restored and can even be rented as unusual overnight stays. On foot, it's reached via the steep PR12 Vereda do Paço trail; from the water, it's simply another cove along the coastline.

Can you visit Fajã dos Padres by boat?

Yes, and by boat is genuinely the easiest way in. Fajã dos Padres — "terrace of the priests" — sits on a shelf of land created by an old volcanic landslide beneath sheer cliffs, and is normally reached by cable car or boat, since there is no road down. The water here is some of the clearest on Madeira's south coast, which makes it a natural stop for swimming and snorkeling as well as sightseeing.

Are the São Vicente Caves the same as Madeira's sea caves?

No — they're a different attraction entirely, and it's a common mix-up. The São Vicente Caves, on the north coast, are volcanic lava tubes formed underground roughly 890,000 years ago and explored on foot with a guide. Madeira's sea caves, by contrast, sit at sea level along the south coast and are shaped by waves and human hands, not lava flow. If you want to stand inside a cave, head to São Vicente; if you want to see caves carved into cliffs from the water, head south.

How do you actually get to Madeira's sea caves?

For most of this stretch of coastline, a boat is the only way to see it properly. The cliffs above Calhau da Lapa and Fajã dos Padres have no coastal road, so a car only gets you to a viewpoint far above; a boat gets you right up to the base of the cliffs, into the coves, and close enough to see the caves at eye level. It's exactly the kind of coastline our Hidden Coves half-day trip was built around — slow past Cabo Girão, into the quiet water off Fajã dos Padres and Calhau da Lapa, with time to swim before heading back to Funchal Marina.

When is the best time to explore Madeira's coastline by boat?

Late spring through early autumn gives the calmest sea state and the clearest water for seeing into the coves and caves from the surface. Mornings tend to have the flattest water before the afternoon breeze picks up, and the low, angled light in the first hours after sunrise brings out the texture of the basalt cliffs far better than the flat glare of midday.

What should you bring on a sea cave boat trip?

A swimsuit and a mask if you want to get in the water at Fajã dos Padres or a quiet cove nearby, a light layer for the breeze once the boat is moving, and a phone or camera with a wide lens — the cliffs at Cabo Girão and around Calhau da Lapa are hard to fit in frame from close up. Reef-safe sunscreen is worth packing too, since much of this stretch is unshaded open water.

The caves aren't a detour from the coastline — they're the reason to see it from the water in the first place.

Most visitors see Cabo Girão once, from the skywalk, and never learn what's underneath it. The caves, the fajãs and the clear water beneath the cliffs are the part of Madeira that only opens up once you're on the sea.
