Top 10 Viewpoints in Madeira

30 June 20266 min readFunchal · Madeira

Madeira rises from sea level to 1,862 metres in under 20 kilometres — which means its viewpoints aren't just pretty; they're genuinely revelatory. Here are the ten that are worth rearranging your itinerary for.

1. Cabo Girão from the water — the view nobody expects

Most visitors see Cabo Girão from the glass-floored skywalk on top. But the cliff only reveals its true scale from below — 580 metres of vertical volcanic basalt, one of the highest sea cliffs in Europe, plunging straight into the Atlantic. From land you look down at the ocean; from a boat you look up at the sky. The difference is staggering, and no photograph quite captures the silence of being anchored beneath that wall. It is the single view that makes a private coastal boat trip from Funchal worth it on its own.

2. Miradouro do Cabo do Girão — the glass skywalk

At 580 metres, the official viewpoint at Cabo Girão is one of the highest sea-cliff viewpoints in Europe. The glass-floored section lets you look straight down to the terraced vineyards and tiny fishing boats working the water below. Free to enter, open daily, and consistently one of the most photographed spots on the island. Go early — before 9am — to beat the tour coaches and get the clearest light.

3. Pico do Arieiro at sunrise

Madeira's third-highest peak at 1,818 metres is reachable by road — one of the few high-altitude summits in Portugal you can drive to. Arrive before dawn and you'll watch the sun rise above a sea of cloud, the island's lower valleys completely swallowed in white. The light between 6:30 and 7:30am in summer is extraordinary. Bring warm layers: the summit is typically 10–15°C cooler than Funchal, and wind is common year-round.

4. Miradouro dos Balcões — the valley amphitheatre

A 3.5 km return walk from Ribeiro Frio along a flat levada leads to one of the best valley viewpoints in central Madeira. The platform looks across a deep natural amphitheatre of peaks — on clear mornings, Pico Ruivo and Pico do Arieiro are visible simultaneously. Chaffinches here are famously tame and will land on your hand for seeds (bring some). Allow 1.5 hours return and go in the morning before cloud builds up in the valleys.

5. Eira do Serrado — looking down into Curral das Freiras

The Nuns' Valley sits inside a volcanic caldera ringed by thousand-metre cliffs — so remote that the nuns of Funchal hid here when Barbary pirates raided the city in 1566. The viewpoint at Eira do Serrado (around 1,000 metres) looks almost straight down into the village 400 metres below. Drive up from Funchal in 25 minutes, or walk down to the village and back up for the full experience. The local chestnut soup is worth the detour.

6. Ponta de São Lourenço — the eastern peninsula

Madeira's eastern tip is geologically unlike anywhere else on the island — bare, wind-sculpted rock tapering out into the Atlantic. The 8 km return hiking trail ends at a viewpoint looking back along the full length of Madeira, with Porto Santo visible in the far distance on a clear day. Start mid-morning and bring water and sun protection; there is almost no shade. The best light falls in the afternoon when it hits the peninsula from the west.

7. Véu da Noiva — north coast waterfall viewpoint

On the north coast between São Vicente and Porto Moniz, a short roadside stop frames the Véu da Noiva (Bridal Veil) waterfall cascading directly into the sea. The surrounding basalt cliffs are draped in ferns and mosses that don't exist on the drier south coast — a completely different Madeira. No hiking required; this is a five-minute stop from the coast road and worth building into any north coast drive.

8. Fanal in the Laurisilva — fog and ancient trees

Fanal, on the Paul da Serra plateau, is more mood than panorama. The twisted til and laurel trees are frequently wrapped in cloud, making the forest feel prehistoric. When the mist clears, you get open views across the plateau and far down to the sea. This is part of Madeira's UNESCO-protected Laurisilva laurel forest — a relic of the subtropical forests that covered southern Europe before the last ice age. Late afternoon light here is unlike anything else on the island.

9. Câmara de Lobos from the sea

Winston Churchill considered this fishing village's view — boats stacked above a black-sand cove, cliffs rising on three sides, open sea ahead — one of the most beautiful in Europe. He painted it from the harbour wall. Seeing it from the water reverses the perspective entirely: you become part of the view the village looks out at. Chifbay's coastal discovery route passes directly through Câmara de Lobos, and this is reliably the moment on every trip when people go quiet.

10. Sunset on the open Atlantic

The best viewpoint in Madeira isn't a miradouro at all. Facing west from the water as the sun drops below the horizon — Funchal's lights beginning to appear on the hillside behind you, the sky turning from orange to deep purple — is an experience no clifftop can replicate. No fence, no coach park, no railing between you and the view. Just open ocean and changing light.

The island is beautiful from the road. It's extraordinary from the water.

Most of these viewpoints can be combined across two or three days on land. Build in a half-day at sea — the perspective it gives to everything else you'll see from the miradouros is worth more than another hour on the road.
