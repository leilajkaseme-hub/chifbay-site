Where to Stay in Madeira: Funchal or the Coastal Villages?

1 July 20267 min readFunchal · Madeira

Funchal is the right base for most trips to Madeira — but not every trip. Here's an honest breakdown of Funchal versus the island's coastal villages, and who each one actually suits.

Is it better to stay in Funchal or elsewhere in Madeira?

For a first visit of four to seven nights, Funchal is the better base. It's the only town with the airport transfer on your doorstep, a working marina, an old town packed with restaurants, and everything else in Madeira reachable as a day trip. The coastal villages are worth it too, but they suit a specific traveller — someone with a car, a slower pace in mind, and usually a second Madeira trip already behind them.

What's it actually like staying in Funchal?

Funchal is a proper small city — a working harbour, a cable car up to Monte, a market (Mercado dos Lavradores) that's worth a morning on its own, and a seafront promenade lined with restaurants. You can walk from most central hotels to the marina, the old town's Zona Velha, and the cathedral in under fifteen minutes. It's also the only base where you don't need a rental car for at least half your trip — taxis, buses and short walks cover the essentials, and you can add a car for a day or two to reach the mountains or the north coast.

Is Funchal too touristy?

Parts of it, yes — the main strip along Avenida do Mar and the cable car queue in high season feel like any resort town. But Funchal is a real Portuguese city of over 100,000 people, not a purpose-built resort, and its old town, market and back streets stay genuinely local a five-minute walk from the busiest points. If you want quiet, book a hotel slightly up the hillside rather than avoiding Funchal altogether — you keep the convenience and lose the crowds.

What are the best coastal towns outside Funchal?

Câmara de Lobos, Ponta do Sol and Calheta are the three worth knowing. Câmara de Lobos is a working fishing village ten minutes from Funchal by car, with brightly painted boats and a clifftop view toward Cabo Girão — close enough to use as a Funchal alternative without losing the city's convenience. Ponta do Sol, about 30 minutes west, is Madeira's unofficial "digital nomad village," a small sunny town with a free co-working space, colourful old streets and a pebble beach, popular with remote workers staying a month rather than a week. Calheta, roughly 40 minutes from Funchal, has the island's best sand beach — a partly imported-sand bay backed by volcanic cliffs — and is often the sunniest spot on Madeira when the capital is under cloud.

Do you need a car if you stay outside Funchal?

Yes, essentially. Public transport between Madeira's towns is thin, and ride-hailing apps are unreliable once you're outside Funchal, so a rental car is close to a requirement if you base yourself in Câmara de Lobos, Ponta do Sol or Calheta. Factor in Madeira's mountain roads and tunnels too — routes that look short on a map can take longer than expected, especially after dark.

Where should you stay if a boat trip is the priority?

Stay within walking distance of Marina do Funchal. It's the departure point for private charters, and staying nearby means no drive back after a sunset cruise or a full day on the water — you step off the boat and you're already close to dinner. It's one of the reasons most guests booking a private boat trip with Chifbay choose central Funchal over the villages, even for trips that also include a night or two on the coast.

Which base suits which traveller?

Couples and first-time visitors do best in central Funchal, close to the marina and old town. Families often prefer the hillside hotels just above the centre, which trade a short walk for pools and quiet. Remote workers and longer stays suit Ponta do Sol, with its co-working space and lower cost of living. Beach-focused trips point to Calheta. And anyone splitting a week across two bases typically books Funchal for the arrival and departure nights and a village for the days in between — getting the airport convenience and the quieter coast without compromising on either.

Funchal gets you everywhere. The villages get you somewhere quieter. Most good trips use both.

Wherever you sleep, the marina is the one fixed point worth building a day around — it's the easiest way to see the coastline that no road on the island actually reaches.
